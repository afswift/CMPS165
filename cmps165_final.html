Global Waste

We originally are focused upon trying to understand the inequalities between the First World and Third, the Global North and South, the West and East, et cetera, those who have, and those who do not. We might assume immediately that developed countries from industrializing, industrialized, and post-industrial, might have some pattern of consumption - the rich countries can consume more, and the poorer countries don't consume as much, or cannot afford to be wasteful.

We achieve conclusions that approach our hypothesis moderately closely, but not quite. In looking at total waste, the countries that do produce the most tend to be the largest, most populated ones; the powerhouses, we'll say; The U.S. and China produce the most waste of all countries in the world, followed up closely by Brazil, Russia, India, and Germany. Some of them are post-industrial, some of them are industrial powers, and some are industrializing. Fortunately, we can look closely at Waste Per Capita (i.e., how many kilograms of aste a person in such country produces in a given day). Then, our hypotheses are confirmed some more.

The West tends to contain the countries with the highest WPC ratings. America and Canada produce disproportionately high amounts of waste per capita, and waste tends to spike more towards one end of the iron curtain than another. But, this analysis yields some anomalies; unexpected countries like Belize outrank the U.S. in consumption by a few grams per capita, while places like Sri Lanka or Kuait far outrank other Western countries.

These unique cases would definitely be interesting to look at, in that these edge cases might show the limits of geopolitical/national processes; one specific edge caseis the nation of Costa Rica. In learning Latin American national history in History 11A with Frank O'Hara in Fall 2014, Costa Rica was shown off as an edge case, lacking the general instability Latin America maintained within a some post-colonial period. A hypothesized explanation was that this instability was due to Costa Rica lacking a history of extractive colonialism, and land being distributed comparatively fairly and equitably.

What does this say about Belize or Sri Lanka? perhaps they have to import more goods,, and generate more waste based on packaging. Maybe somehow the people in those countries live lifestyles comfortable with disposability, and if it is sustainable somehow, perhaps we can model their practices when it comes to our own practices.

Inequalities between the Global Rich and Poor are also shown off, although not as strongly. Poorer countries have the tendency to generate more organic waste, and less metal or plastic (although Iran is an interesting edge case that I'd like to have answers about. How on Earth does Sweden generate so much paper waste? What similarities are there between the countries that failed to provide breakdowns of their waste?
